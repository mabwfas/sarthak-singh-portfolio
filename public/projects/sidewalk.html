Sidewalk — Small Business Social & Messaging Platform

How we built an interactive community platform that connects local businesses with real-time messaging, social feeds, and business discovery — all in 8 weeks.

Role: iOS Developer
Duration: 8 weeks
Published: Dec 2019

The Client

Sidewalk Inc.

Sidewalk Inc. wanted to create an interactive platform for local businesses to communicate with one another, share best practices, and discover vendors and other businesses in their community. The vision was clear: any business, large or small, could set up an account in a few clicks to begin discovering what others around them are doing.

The founding team had deep roots in the small business community and saw firsthand how isolated many local businesses felt. They believed that if you could lower the barrier to connection — making it as simple as signing up and browsing a feed — businesses would naturally form the kind of partnerships and knowledge-sharing networks that help them thrive.

The Challenge

Small Businesses Had No Dedicated Place to Connect

Small businesses struggled to connect meaningfully with their local community. Social media platforms like Facebook and Instagram were too broad, algorithm-driven, and expensive for neighborhood-level engagement. There was no dedicated space where local businesses could build genuine professional relationships at the community level.

  • ✕
    No discovery tools for local businesses — Finding tools, vendors, and partnership opportunities among other companies in the area required word-of-mouth or expensive networking events.
  • ✕
    No relevant, localized business feeds — Existing social platforms buried local business content under algorithm-driven consumer posts. There was no way to stay up to date on everything business within local and categorized news feeds.
  • ✕
    No direct communication channel — Businesses had no way to form one-on-one relationships with other companies and communicate directly in a professional, business-focused context.
My Approach

Start to Finish — The Complete Build Journey

Here is exactly how I took Sidewalk from concept to App Store in 8 weeks, broken down phase by phase.

Phase 1 — Week 1
Discovery & Planning

The first week was dedicated entirely to understanding the product vision and mapping out the technical foundation.

  • Stakeholder meetings — Met with the Sidewalk team to understand their vision for the platform, target users, and success metrics. Discussed the competitive landscape and what would make Sidewalk different from general social media.
  • User journey mapping — Mapped out the three core user journeys in detail: business discovery (finding vendors, tools, and partners), feed browsing (staying informed on local business activity), and direct messaging (forming one-on-one business relationships).
  • Information architecture — Designed the data architecture for business profiles, social feeds, messaging threads, and user authentication. Created entity-relationship diagrams to ensure efficient data modeling.
  • Technology stack selection — Chose Swift/UIKit for native iOS development (performance and polish), Firebase Realtime Database for the real-time backend (instant sync for messaging and feeds), and AWS for additional infrastructure services.
Phase 2 — Week 2
Architecture & Setup

With the plan locked in, I set up the project scaffolding and all backend services.

  • Project setup — Initialized the Xcode project with Swift and UIKit, following the MVVM (Model-View-ViewModel) architecture pattern to ensure clean separation of concerns and testable business logic.
  • Firebase configuration — Set up Firebase Realtime Database for real-time data synchronization across all clients. Configured database rules for security and structured the JSON tree for efficient queries.
  • Authentication layer — Implemented Firebase Authentication with email/password registration, ensuring secure user onboarding. Built password reset flows and session management.
  • AWS integration — Configured AWS services for additional backend infrastructure, including media storage and server-side processing that Firebase alone couldn't handle.
  • Data models — Created the core Swift data models for businesses, posts, messages, user profiles, and categories. Ensured all models were Codable for seamless Firebase serialization.
Phase 3 — Weeks 3 to 5
Core Feature Development

The bulk of the build happened during these three weeks. Every major feature came to life.

  • Business Profiles — Built comprehensive business profile pages with service listings, location display via MapKit, contact information, media gallery, and business category tagging. Each profile served as a mini-website for the business within the app.
  • Social Feed System — Created local and categorized news feeds where businesses could post updates, promotions, events, and announcements. Implemented real-time feed updates so new posts appeared instantly without manual refresh. Added support for text, image, and link posts.
  • Business Discovery — Implemented in-app search and discovery features with filtering by category, location, and business type. Used MapKit to show nearby businesses on an interactive map, letting users explore their local business ecosystem visually.
  • Direct Messaging — Built a full real-time one-on-one messaging system using Firebase Realtime Database. Messages appeared instantly on both ends with read receipts, typing indicators, and support for rich media (images and links). This was the core engagement feature of the app.
  • Push Notifications — Integrated Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) for push notifications. Configured notifications for new messages, feed interactions, and new business activity in the user's area to drive ongoing engagement.
Phase 4 — Weeks 6 to 7
Polish & Testing

With all features functional, I focused on making the app feel polished, performant, and bulletproof.

  • UI refinement — Refined every screen to match the client's design specifications pixel-perfectly. Adjusted spacing, typography, colors, and layout to ensure a professional, cohesive visual identity throughout the app.
  • Animations and transitions — Implemented smooth animations for screen transitions, message bubbles, feed loading, and interactive elements. Added subtle micro-interactions that gave the app a premium, responsive feel.
  • Device testing — Conducted comprehensive testing across multiple iPhone models (iPhone 8 through iPhone 11 Pro Max at the time) to ensure layout consistency, performance, and usability on every screen size.
  • Edge case handling — Fixed edge cases in real-time messaging (offline message queuing, simultaneous typing, message ordering) and data synchronization (conflict resolution, network loss recovery).
  • Performance optimization — Profiled and optimized app performance including memory usage, image loading (lazy loading and caching), feed pagination, and Firebase query efficiency. Ensured smooth 60fps scrolling and fast screen loads.
Phase 5 — Week 8
Launch & Delivery

The final stretch: preparing for the App Store and shipping the product.

  • App Store preparation — Created polished App Store screenshots for all required device sizes, wrote the app description with optimized keywords, and prepared all metadata for Apple's requirements.
  • Apple App Review submission — Submitted the app to Apple's review process, ensuring full compliance with the App Store Review Guidelines. Addressed any review feedback promptly.
  • Successful launch — Sidewalk went live on the App Store, ready for businesses to download and start connecting with their local community.
  • Documentation and handoff — Provided complete project documentation including architecture overview, setup instructions, API documentation, Firebase structure guide, and deployment procedures so the client's team could maintain and extend the app going forward.

Key Technical Decisions

Decision 01

Native Swift over React Native

Chose native iOS development with Swift for optimal performance, smooth 60fps animations, and full access to iOS platform APIs like MapKit, push notifications, and camera. For an app that relied heavily on real-time messaging and map interactions, native development ensured the polished, responsive experience that users expect.

Decision 02

Firebase Realtime Database for Live Features

Firebase Realtime Database was the perfect choice for the core messaging and live feed features. It provides instant data synchronization across all connected clients without polling — when one user sends a message or posts an update, every relevant client sees it in milliseconds. This eliminated the need to build and maintain a custom WebSocket server.

Decision 03

MVVM Architecture Pattern

Adopted the Model-View-ViewModel pattern to separate business logic from the UI layer. This made the codebase maintainable and testable — ViewModels could be unit tested independently of UIKit, and the clear separation made it easy for the client's future developers to understand and extend the code.

Decision 04

MapKit for Business Location Features

Used Apple's native MapKit framework for all business location and discovery features. MapKit provided smooth, familiar map interactions that iOS users already understand, with excellent performance and no additional API costs. Custom annotations and clustering made the map-based discovery experience intuitive and visually clean.
